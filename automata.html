Cellular Automaton Background

What is a Cellular Automaton?

A cellular automaton (CA) is a simple computational model consisting of a grid of cells. Each cell can be in one of a finite number of states (like 'alive' or 'dead', represented here by gray or white). The grid evolves over discrete time steps.

At each time step, the state of every cell is updated simultaneously based on a fixed set of rules. These rules depend on the cell's own current state and the states of its neighboring cells. Famous examples include Conway's Game of Life.

Rules for This Automaton

The specific automaton running in the background follows these custom rules, applied to each cell at every time step:

  • Survival: A 'live' (gray) cell with exactly 2 or 3 live neighbors survives to the next generation. Otherwise, it dies (becomes white).
  • Birth: A 'dead' (white) cell with exactly 3 or 6 live neighbors becomes 'live' (gray) in the next generation. Otherwise, it remains dead.

Neighbors are the eight cells directly surrounding a given cell (horizontally, vertically, and diagonally). The grid wraps around edges, so cells on the edge consider neighbors on the opposite side.

This specific rule set (often referred to as B36/S23) is similar but not identical to Conway's Game of Life B3/S23 and can generate complex patterns and structures from simple initial random states.
